I still believe education is one of the best investments a person can make for a better life.

I do not mean education only as a degree, a certificate, or a line on a resume. Those things can matter, especially early in life, because they open doors and create signals. But the deeper value of education is not the paper. It is the ability to think more clearly, learn faster, make better choices, and participate in more kinds of work.

When I speak with students or parents, I often notice two opposite worries. One side worries that education has become too expensive, too competitive, or too disconnected from jobs. The other side worries that AI will make many careers uncertain, so maybe traditional learning does not matter as much. Both worries are understandable. But neither is a good reason to treat education casually.

The world is changing, but that makes learning more important, not less.

## Education Builds Options

The first value of education is option creation.

A good education gives a young person more paths to choose from. It may help them get into a better college, qualify for a better first job, understand technology, start a business, move across industries, or simply avoid being trapped by the first opportunity available to them.

Income is part of this, but it is not the whole story. Education can also create confidence. It can create independence. It can help someone speak up in a room, understand contracts, evaluate advice, question bad assumptions, and notice when they are being manipulated.

In my own work, I have seen people with different degrees, backgrounds, and starting points grow into strong professionals because they kept learning. I have also seen people with impressive credentials stop growing because they treated education as something that ended after graduation. The difference is rarely only intelligence. It is usually the habit of learning, the willingness to do hard things, and the ability to connect knowledge with real work.

## Education Is Not Just Schooling

School is one part of education. It is not the whole system.

Students still need strong fundamentals: language, mathematics, science, history, economics, technology, and the ability to write clearly. These give the mind structure. They help people understand cause and effect. They make it easier to learn specialized skills later.

But young people also need practice outside exams. They need to build things, explain ideas, work with others, ask questions, make mistakes, and finish projects. A student who can take a messy problem, break it down, learn what is missing, and produce something useful has an advantage in almost any field.

Parents sometimes ask whether their child should focus on marks or skills. My answer is usually: do not turn it into a false choice. Marks matter because they reflect discipline and can unlock opportunities. Skills matter because life and work will test what you can actually do. The goal is not to worship either one. The goal is to build capability.

## AI Raises the Bar for Human Ability

AI will change many jobs. Some tasks will become easier. Some will be automated. Some roles will shrink, and new roles will appear. But AI does not remove the need for educated people. It increases the need for people who can think, judge, communicate, and learn continuously.

If a tool can produce a first draft, the human must know whether it is correct. If a tool can generate code, the engineer must understand architecture, tradeoffs, testing, security, and maintainability. If a tool can summarize information, the professional must still know what matters, what is missing, and what decision follows.

This is where education becomes more than content memorization. It becomes judgment.

The students who do well in the AI era will not be the ones who only know how to use tools. Many people will know how to use tools. The stronger advantage will belong to people who understand domains deeply enough to use tools responsibly and creatively.

## What Parents Can Do

Parents do not need to predict the exact job market of 2035. None of us can do that with confidence.

What parents can do is help children build durable foundations. Encourage reading. Encourage numeracy. Encourage curiosity. Encourage building. Encourage clear speech and clear writing. Encourage responsibility. Help them learn technology, but do not let technology replace effort.

I would also tell parents to avoid panic-driven choices. Do not push a child into a field only because it sounds safe this year. Safe fields change. Fashionable fields become crowded. What lasts longer is the ability to learn, adapt, work with discipline, and solve real problems.

## The Real Investment

The real investment in education is not only fees, tuition, or time. It is attention.

It is the attention a student gives to becoming capable. It is the attention a parent gives to building the right environment. It is the attention a teacher gives to making a subject come alive. It is the attention a professional gives to continuing to learn after the world stops giving exams.

Education is still worth it because life rewards people who can keep expanding what they understand and what they can do.

It does not guarantee success. Nothing does. But it improves the odds in a way very few things can.

For students, it creates opportunity. For parents, it creates confidence that their children can stand on their own feet. For professionals, it creates the ability to keep moving when industries change.

That is why I still see education as one of the strongest ways to build a better life.
