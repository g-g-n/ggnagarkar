If I were preparing a 15-year-old for 2035, I would not try to predict every job title that will exist.

That is a tempting exercise, but it is not very useful. Ten years is enough time for industries, tools, and hiring expectations to change. AI will reshape many tasks. Some jobs will become more automated. Some will become more creative. Some will combine disciplines that are still treated separately today.

So I would prepare the young person for change itself.

Not with fear. Not with pressure. Not with a long list of things they must master immediately. I would build foundations, habits, judgment, and confidence.

## I Would Protect Curiosity

At 15, curiosity is still alive, but it is often under pressure from exams, comparison, and anxiety about the future.

I would protect that curiosity. I would encourage the student to ask how things work, why people behave the way they do, how businesses make money, how software gets built, how machines learn, how cities function, how stories persuade, and how science changes what we believe.

Curiosity is not a distraction from career preparation. It is part of it.

The future will reward people who can learn new domains quickly. Curiosity creates that energy. A student who is curious can enter a new field and start asking useful questions. A student who only studies for marks may struggle when the answer is not in the textbook.

## I Would Build Reading and Writing

I would make reading and writing non-negotiable.

Reading builds vocabulary, attention, context, and judgment. It exposes a young person to people, places, ideas, mistakes, and histories they cannot experience directly. Writing forces clarity. It reveals whether the student actually understands what they think they understand.

In 2035, AI may generate text easily. That does not make writing less important. It makes human judgment about writing more important. The student must know what is clear, what is shallow, what is dishonest, what is persuasive, and what is missing.

I would ask a 15-year-old to write regularly: summaries, arguments, project notes, reflections, and explanations. Not for perfection. For thinking.

## I Would Teach Technology Without Worshipping It

I would want the student to understand AI, programming, data, networks, devices, and automation. Technology will be part of almost every serious career.

But I would not teach technology as magic.

I would teach it as a set of tools built by people, with limits, costs, incentives, and failure modes. AI can help draft, analyze, code, translate, tutor, and automate. It can also hallucinate, inherit bias, leak private information, and create false confidence.

A young person should learn how to use AI well, but also how to question it. They should learn prompting, but also verification. They should learn coding, but also testing. They should learn automation, but also responsibility.

## I Would Encourage Building

I would ask the student to build things.

Small things are enough: a website, a science experiment, a budgeting spreadsheet, a short video essay, a simple app, a local survey, a tutoring plan, a garden sensor, a community project, a repair log, a book club, a prototype.

Building teaches lessons that lectures cannot. It teaches planning, frustration, debugging, compromise, communication, and completion. It also builds identity. A young person who has made things begins to see themselves as capable of shaping the world, not just competing inside it.

By 2035, proof of ability will matter more. Projects create proof.

## I Would Develop Social Confidence

Future jobs will not only reward technical ability. They will reward the ability to work with people.

I would help the student practice speaking clearly, listening carefully, asking for feedback, disagreeing respectfully, and explaining ideas to different audiences. I would encourage teamwork, sports, volunteering, debate, theater, part-time work, or any environment where they must deal with real people and real responsibilities.

Many careers are limited not by intelligence but by poor communication, low confidence, or inability to collaborate.

These skills are easier to build early than to repair later.

## I Would Teach Money and Work

I would want a 15-year-old to understand money in a practical way.

Not greed. Not status. Just reality.

They should understand income, expenses, saving, debt, compounding, risk, taxes, and how businesses create value. They should understand that work is not only about passion. It is also about usefulness, reliability, trust, and solving problems someone cares enough to pay for.

This helps students make better career choices. It also protects them from unrealistic advice.

## I Would Keep the Human Center

Most importantly, I would not make preparation for 2035 only about jobs.

A young person also needs health, friendships, values, resilience, and a sense of meaning. They need to know how to recover from failure. They need to learn that comparison is endless and not always useful. They need adults who believe in them without pretending the world is easy.

The future will be demanding. AI will change workflows. Competition will remain. But a well-prepared student is not someone who knows every future tool. It is someone who can learn, think, communicate, build, adapt, and act with judgment.

That is what I would prepare for.

Not a fixed career path, but a strong person who can keep growing as the path changes.
