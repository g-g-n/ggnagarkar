Degrees matter, especially at the beginning of a career. I do not dismiss them.

A degree can open doors. It can signal discipline. It can create access to networks, interviews, and opportunities that may otherwise be difficult to reach. For many families, a degree also carries emotional and economic weight because it represents years of effort and sacrifice.

But a degree is not the same as capability.

In hiring, building teams, and working with engineers and product people over the years, I have learned to look for skills that are deeper than credentials. Some people have strong degrees but struggle to produce useful work. Some people come from less obvious backgrounds but grow quickly because they have the right habits.

The difference often comes down to a few practical skills.

## Communication

Communication is one of the most underrated career skills.

A person who can explain a problem clearly saves time for everyone around them. They can write a useful status update, ask a precise question, document a decision, summarize a customer issue, and tell the truth without creating confusion.

In technical work, communication is not separate from engineering. A badly explained problem often becomes a badly solved problem. A vague requirement becomes rework. A silent risk becomes a production issue. A team that cannot communicate cannot move fast for very long.

Students should practice writing and speaking as seriously as they practice technical subjects. Write project notes. Explain what you built. Summarize what you learned. Teach someone else. If you cannot explain your thinking, it is usually not clear enough yet.

## Curiosity

Curiosity is not just asking random questions. It is the habit of wanting to understand how things actually work.

Curious people do not stop at surface answers. They ask why a system failed, why customers behave a certain way, why a design choice was made, why a business model works, or why a shortcut creates risk.

This matters because most real work is not textbook-shaped. Problems are messy. Information is incomplete. People disagree. Tools behave strangely. A curious person keeps investigating instead of waiting to be told every step.

Curiosity also protects people from becoming outdated. The person who keeps learning voluntarily has a better chance of adapting when technology changes.

## Problem-Solving

Problem-solving is the ability to move from confusion to clarity.

It includes breaking a large problem into smaller parts, identifying assumptions, testing ideas, using evidence, and knowing when to ask for help. It also includes patience. Many people give up too early because the first answer is not obvious.

In interviews, I care less about whether someone has seen the exact problem before and more about how they approach it. Do they clarify? Do they reason? Do they notice edge cases? Do they change direction when evidence changes? Do they explain tradeoffs?

This skill can be trained. Build projects. Debug real issues. Solve case problems. Read how experienced people think. After completing work, review what happened and ask what you missed.

## Discipline

Discipline sounds old-fashioned, but it is extremely practical.

Discipline is showing up when the work is not exciting. It is finishing tasks properly. It is reading the documentation. It is testing before declaring something done. It is taking notes. It is keeping commitments. It is doing the unglamorous work that makes the visible work reliable.

Many talented people lose momentum because they depend too much on motivation. Motivation comes and goes. Discipline creates consistency.

For students, discipline does not mean living like a machine. It means learning how to manage time, energy, attention, and responsibility. A person who can work steadily has a large advantage over someone who only works in bursts.

## Execution

Execution is where many careers are built.

Ideas are easy to discuss. Plans are easy to make. Execution is the ability to convert intention into finished work that someone can use.

In a job, people remember who gets things done. They remember who can take ownership, handle ambiguity, coordinate with others, and close loops. This is true for engineers, designers, salespeople, managers, founders, and almost every serious role.

Students can build execution by completing small real projects. Do not only start. Finish. Publish. Present. Get feedback. Improve. The habit of completion is powerful.

## Adaptability

Adaptability is not drifting with every trend. It is the ability to keep your foundations stable while updating your methods.

AI is a good example. Some people react with panic. Some ignore it. The adaptable person studies it, uses it, understands where it helps, notices where it fails, and keeps building stronger judgment.

Adaptability requires humility. You must be willing to admit that the way you learned before may not be enough. It also requires confidence. You must believe you can learn the next thing.

## Degrees Plus Skills

The best situation is not degree versus skills. It is degree plus skills.

A good degree can give a student a platform. Strong skills turn that platform into real opportunity. Without skills, the degree becomes fragile. Without some recognized signals, skills may take longer to get noticed. Together, they are powerful.

For students and early-career professionals, the message is simple: do not stop at credentials.

Build the skills employers actually experience when they work with you. Communicate clearly. Stay curious. Solve problems. Be disciplined. Execute. Adapt.

These are not slogans. They are daily behaviors.

Over time, they become reputation. And reputation often matters more than the line printed on a certificate.
