When I hire engineers, I am not only looking for someone who can answer technical questions.

Technical ability matters. It matters a lot. But engineering work in a real company is not an exam. It is a mix of problem-solving, judgment, communication, ownership, collaboration, and the ability to keep systems working after the first version is built.

Over the years, I have learned to look for signals that go beyond the resume.

## Can They Understand the Problem?

The first thing I look for is whether the person tries to understand the problem before solving it.

Some candidates jump straight into an answer. They assume requirements. They start coding before clarifying. They optimize the wrong thing. This can happen in interviews, and it also happens in real projects.

Good engineers ask questions. What is the user trying to do? What are the constraints? What does success mean? What tradeoffs are acceptable? What happens if the system fails? What data do we have? What is unknown?

This does not mean they need to ask endless questions. It means they know that engineering is not just implementation. It is understanding.

## Can They Break Work Down?

Strong engineers can take a messy problem and create structure.

They identify parts, dependencies, risks, assumptions, and phases. They know how to build a small working version before trying to build the perfect final version. They can explain what they would do first, what can wait, and where the hard parts are likely to be.

This matters because real engineering work is rarely clean. Product requirements change. Infrastructure has limits. Customers behave unexpectedly. Deadlines exist. A good engineer brings order without pretending everything is simple.

In interviews, I pay attention to how someone organizes their thinking. The final answer matters, but the path matters too.

## Do They Care About Quality?

I look for engineers who care about quality without becoming impractical.

Quality does not mean gold-plating every feature. It means thinking about correctness, testing, maintainability, security, performance, observability, and the next person who will read the code.

An engineer who never mentions tests worries me. An engineer who never thinks about failure worries me. An engineer who treats production as someone else's problem worries me.

At the same time, quality must be balanced with delivery. Business needs matter. Users need progress. The best engineers understand tradeoffs. They can say, "For this stage, this is enough, but here is the risk and here is what we should improve next."

That judgment is valuable.

## Can They Communicate Clearly?

Communication is a major hiring signal for me.

An engineer does not need to be loud or polished. They do need to be clear. They should be able to explain what they are building, where they are stuck, what changed, what risks exist, and what help they need.

Most engineering failures are not pure coding failures. Many are coordination failures, assumption failures, or silence failures.

A clear engineer helps the whole team move faster. They write useful pull request notes. They document decisions. They surface blockers early. They explain technical choices to non-technical people without arrogance.

This is especially important in remote or distributed teams.

## How Do They Respond When Stuck?

Everyone gets stuck. I care about what happens next.

Some people freeze. Some hide. Some guess. Some blame the tool, the codebase, the previous team, or the requirement. Strong engineers investigate. They form hypotheses. They read logs. They reproduce issues. They ask for help with context. They are honest about what they know and what they do not know.

This is one of the clearest signs of maturity.

In production work, being stuck is normal. The difference is whether a person can move responsibly through uncertainty.

## Do They Take Ownership?

Ownership is not about working unhealthy hours or saying yes to everything.

Ownership means caring about the outcome. If an engineer owns a feature, they think about whether it works for the user, whether it can be operated, whether edge cases are handled, and whether the team understands what changed.

I value people who close loops. If they say they will check something, they come back with an answer. If they find a risk, they raise it. If they make a mistake, they own it and help fix it.

Ownership builds trust, and trust is the foundation of strong teams.

## Are They Still Learning?

Technology changes constantly, especially now with AI changing software development workflows.

I look for engineers who are still learning. Not people who chase every trend, but people who are curious enough to improve. They read, experiment, ask questions, study failures, and update their views.

AI coding tools make this more important. If an engineer uses AI to move faster but does not understand the output, that is risk. If they use AI as a partner while maintaining judgment, testing, and ownership, that can be powerful.

The engineer's learning posture matters more than the exact tool list on their resume.

## The Combined Signal

When hiring engineers, I am looking for a combined signal:

- Can they think clearly?
- Can they build?
- Can they communicate?
- Can they handle ambiguity?
- Can they care about quality?
- Can they learn?
- Can the team trust them?

No candidate is perfect. I do not expect perfection. I do expect evidence of capability and growth.

For students and early-career engineers, this is good news. You do not need to pretend you know everything. You need to show how you think, how you learn, how you take responsibility, and how you turn problems into working systems.

That is what I look for when hiring engineers.
